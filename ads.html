THE PROBLEM

With the current user interface, users of Campaign Manager and Display & Video 360 have to understand a large amount of complicated, ads-related jargon. As a result, users were filing tickets because they couldn't onboard as quickly as they would like. This is due to several reasons:

  1. Lack of context within the product
  2. Inconsistent use of terminology or concepts across products
  3. Outdated or unclear documentation
fingerprint

THE SOLUTION

UX writing for new features

I wrote the in-UI and email content for a new feature related to bulk action in Campaign Manager 360. I collaborated with the teams' software engineers, interaction designers, and product technology manager to decide on the product direction of the feature.

Deliverables included naming the feature, drafts of email content and in-UI text. The purpose of this was to communicate to the user the status of their bulk action in order to provide consistent feedback. Challenges included maintaining consistency between the UI, emails, and any downloaded content.

I also drafted the UX writing for an onboarding flow to a new feature in Google Ads. The process included researching onboarding flow patterns, studying existing flows, and getting feedback from stakeholders in the project.

Advocating for in-product education

To advocate for in-product education and long-term documentation changes, I developed a qualitative review for an existing article on modeled conversions.↗ This article in particular is important because it addresses user privacy and data. This topic is inconsistently used and referenced across multiple Ads products, negatively impacting a large number of users. I presented my findings to product stakeholders to publish the changes, which consolidated the information cross-product and clarified the Google's approach to conversion modeling.

I also worked hands-on project for the reporting feature. Users could choose metrics and dimensions in order to generate reports and understand the effectiveness of their ads. However, many of the metric and dimension names were unfamiliar to users, resulting in them filing tickets. I worked on drafting 50+ descriptions for in-UI tooltips. As a result, users can simply hover over a metric and dimensions to quickly see the definition.

Documenting product launches

For reporting, I supported 3 larger product launches, 3P App Attribution, reporting quotas, and data downloads in instant reporting.

I communicated regularly with software engineers, interaction designers, and product technology managers to gain understanding of the feature. My primary goal was to identify the key information to include without overwhelming the user with information.

LEARNINGS

Technical writing is, at its core, education. We are teaching users about our products, beyond just how to use them. In order to accomplish this goal, I learned that more content isn't always the answer. Sometimes removing information or being intentional about the placement of content can make the main idea more clear. Placing clear, instructional writing within the user interface itself is an effective and accessible way to provide valuable context.

Finally, the time spent researching patterns and speaking to subject matter experts may not yield tangible deliverables, but it's one of the most crucial steps in the technical writing process. I now recognize the importance of this work and learned to communciate the impact of this work to stakeholders.
